How I ship a SaaS MVP in exactly six weeks

Six weeks isn't a deadline. It's a forcing function. The real work isn't deciding what to build — it's deciding what not to. After shipping 40+ of these, here's the exact framework, the features I cut every single time, and why week five is always the one that breaks founders.

Founders come to me with a spec. The spec is always too long. That's not a criticism — it's physics. You've been thinking about your product for months; I've been thinking about it for an hour. Of course your mental model has more branches than mine.

My job, in the first week, is to prune that tree down to what a paying customer actually needs. Not what a pitch deck needs. Not what future you might need when you have 500 customers. What today's first user — the one who hasn't signed up yet — needs to reach a moment of usefulness.

The six-week skeleton

Every MVP runs on the same backbone. The domain varies, the features vary, the scoreline varies — but the cadence is identical. Six weeks broken into four phases:

  • Week 1: Discovery, cuts, and architecture. No code.
  • Weeks 2–3: Auth, billing, admin shell. The boring stuff first.
  • Weeks 4–5: The one feature that matters. The thing a user pays for.
  • Week 6: Polish, deploy, and the launch checklist.

Notice what's missing: there's no "design week," no "user testing sprint," no "iterate on feedback." Those things happen — but they happen concurrently, not in their own box. A six-week MVP doesn't have the runway for sequential quality gates.

The things I cut every single time

Here's the list I bring to every kickoff call. If your spec contains any of these, they're leaving the room. We can add them back in week 7 — after you have paying customers telling you to.

"But customers will expect it." No they won't. They'll expect it if you list it. They won't notice if you don't.

1. Social login

Email + password is fine for an MVP. Sign-in-with-Google costs you a day of integration, a Google Cloud console, an OAuth consent screen that needs review if you go past 100 users, and a dependency that can break on you without warning. Launch with email. Add Google in week 7 if users ask. They will not ask.

2. Password reset by SMS

You don't have 1,000 users yet. You have zero. Email password reset is free, it works, and Twilio costs per message. Ship the email flow. Add SMS when the cost per password reset is less than the cost per signup.

3. In-app notifications

Email is a notification system that already exists, has infinite retention, works on every device, and users read. A bell icon with a badge count is half a week of engineering that buys you nothing a weekly digest email doesn't.

4. Dark mode

Nobody has ever canceled a subscription because a SaaS lacked dark mode. It's a week of CSS variables and QA that doesn't convert a single user. Six months from now, when your product has design DNA worth respecting, do it right. Not now.

The three I refuse to compromise on

Equally important is the list of things I won't ship without. These are the non-negotiables. Skip any of them and the MVP isn't a product, it's a demo:

  1. Billing that can actually charge a card. Not "billing coming soon." Not a Stripe test key. An MVP that can't take money isn't an MVP — it's a prototype with HTML.
  2. A password reset flow that actually works. 30% of your early users will lock themselves out in the first week. If your recovery flow doesn't work, those users are permanently gone.
  3. An admin panel you can use without a database client. Not Forge, not phpMyAdmin, not SSH + MySQL CLI. A real admin panel where you or your support person can see a user, toggle a flag, refund a charge. You will use it every single day.

Why week five is always the hardest

In weeks 1–4, everyone's excited. Auth ships. Billing ships. The shell comes together. Founders get their first look, it's real moment. Then week five arrives and reality hits.

Week five is when the one feature that actually matters — the thing your product is for — collides with the time you have left. Every MVP I've ever done, the core feature is more complex than the spec suggested. Not because founders are wrong. Because the spec only captured the happy path, and production is an obstacle course of edge cases. 

// The naive spec:
user.uploads(receipt)
  .then(r => ocr.extract(r))
  .then(data => db.save(data))

// The actual week 5 reality:
// — what if the receipt is a PDF?
// — what if the photo is upside down?
// — what if OCR returns 3 possible values?
// — what if the user uploads the same receipt twice?
// — what if OCR is down?
// — what if the user cancels mid-upload?

My rule for week five: cut every "nice to have" in the core feature, no exceptions. Every founder wants to push back. Every founder wants to keep "just this one small thing." It's never just one small thing — it's the thing that pushes week six into week eight.

Week six — the launch week

Week six is never about building. If you're still building in week six, you've already slipped. Week six is: deploy, test on real devices, fill the legal pages, set up the transactional emails, run the checkout on a real card, and write the launch post.

I ship on a Wednesday. Not Friday — Friday ships die over the weekend when nobody's watching. Not Monday — Monday is for the team to recover from weekend panic. Wednesday gives you Thursday and Friday to fix what's broken before anyone important is watching.

What's outside the MVP

The question I get most is: "what about all the things we cut?" The answer: they're in version 1.1. An MVP is not a finished product. It's the smallest possible version of your product that can take money and teach you what you got wrong. Everything else is version 1.1, 1.2, 1.3…

If the MVP succeeds, you'll spend the next six months adding back the things you cut — but only the ones that actually mattered. Most of them, it turns out, don't. That's the whole point of the exercise.

The uncomfortable truth

Six weeks is a constraint, not a promise. I promise a shipped product. I don't promise it'll be your vision of the product. It'll be the smallest version of your product that actually works — and in my experience, that's the version your users prefer anyway.

If you're considering a SaaS MVP and the "six weeks" number scares you, that's a good sign. If it sounds comfortable, your spec is too short.
